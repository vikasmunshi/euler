Problem 113: Non-bouncy Numbers [Level 7]

Problem Statement

Working from left-to-right if no digit is exceeded by the digit to its left it is called an increasing number; for example, $134468$.

Similarly if no digit is exceeded by the digit to its right it is called a decreasing number; for example, $66420$.

We shall call a positive integer that is neither increasing nor decreasing a "bouncy" number; for example, $155349$.

As $n$ increases, the proportion of bouncy numbers below $n$ increases such that there are only $12951$ numbers below one-million that are not bouncy and only $277032$ non-bouncy numbers below $10^{10}$.

How many numbers below a googol ($10^{100}$) are not bouncy?

Mathematical Complexity

The core insight is that counting monotone digit sequences maps directly onto combinatorial multiset selection via stars-and-bars. An increasing number of exactly k digits is a non-decreasing sequence chosen from {1, ..., 9} with repetition, which gives C(k+8, 8) choices. A decreasing number of exactly k digits is a non-increasing sequence from {0, ..., 9} with a nonzero leading digit; the full multiset count over ten digits is C(k+9, 9), minus 1 to exclude the all-zeros non-number, giving C(k+9, 9) - 1. Numbers satisfying both conditions simultaneously are repdigits - constant sequences - of which there are exactly 9 for any digit length, so the per-length non-bouncy count is C(k+8, 8) + C(k+9, 9) - 10.

Summing that expression from k = 1 to d and applying the hockey-stick identity ( sum_{k=1}^{d} C(k+r, r) = C(d+r+1, r+1) - 1) to each term collapses the entire loop into a two-term closed form: C(d+9, 9) + C(d+10, 10) - 10d - 2. For the googol case d = 100 the largest intermediate value is C(110, 10), which fits comfortably in a 64-bit integer. The algorithm is effectively O(1) - it requires only two iterative binomial coefficient evaluations each taking at most 10 multiplication steps, regardless of how large d is.

Solution Approach

Closed-form combinatorial formula (Python and C, s0)

I implemented the same algorithm in both p0113_s0.py and p0113_s0.c. The derivation proceeds in three steps, which I preserved as detailed comments in the Python source:

  • Increasing numbers of exactly k digits: C(k+8, 8), counting non-decreasing selections with repetition from {1, ..., 9}.
  • Decreasing numbers of exactly k digits: C(k+9, 9) - 1, subtracting the invalid all-zeros sequence from the multiset count over {0, ..., 9}.
  • Subtract the 9 repdigits per digit length that satisfy both criteria simultaneously, giving the inclusion-exclusion correction of -10 per digit length (the -1 from the decreasing formula and the -9 repdigit overlap combine to -10).

Applying the hockey-stick identity to sum C(k+8, 8) and sum C(k+9, 9) separately produces the closed form C(d+9, 9) + C(d+10, 10) - 10d - 2, which I evaluate in a single expression.

In Python I used math.comb, which handles arbitrarily large integers natively. In C I wrote a small iterative comb function that divides after each multiplication step to prevent intermediate overflow - since k never exceeds 10 in these calls, integer divisibility is guaranteed at each step and the results stay well within long long range.

Learnings

The central insight was recognising that a monotone digit sequence of length k is exactly a multiset selection from a bounded alphabet - once that mapping clicked, stars-and-bars turned a seemingly complex digit-counting problem into a one-line formula. The hockey-stick identity then eliminated even the need for a loop over digit lengths, collapsing the whole summation to two binomial coefficients.

The inclusion-exclusion step for repdigits is a small but important detail: numbers that are both increasing and decreasing form the overlap between the two sets, and subtracting them once per digit length is necessary to avoid double-counting. Forgetting this step would overcount by 9d.

Techniques worth remembering from this problem: stars-and-bars for non-decreasing digit sequences; the hockey-stick identity for collapsing sums of consecutive binomial coefficients into a single term; and the pattern of applying inclusion-exclusion when two monotonicity criteria share a common subset. The general structure - counting sequences with a monotonicity property over a bounded alphabet - appears frequently in combinatorics, and reducing it to a closed form via these identities is far more elegant than a digit dynamic programming approach, which would require maintaining state over all digit lengths up to 100.

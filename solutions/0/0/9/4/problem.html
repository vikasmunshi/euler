Problem 94: Almost Equilateral Triangles [Level 12]

Problem Statement

It is easily proved that no equilateral triangle exists with integral length sides and integral area. However, the almost equilateral triangle $5$-$5$-$6$ has an area of $12$ square units.

We shall define an almost equilateral triangle to be a triangle for which two sides are equal and the third differs by no more than one unit.

Find the sum of the perimeters of all almost equilateral triangles with integral side lengths and area and whose perimeters do not exceed one billion ($1\,000\,000\,000$).

Mathematical Complexity

An almost equilateral triangle is isosceles with equal sides of length a and a base of either a+1 or a-1. Applying Heron's formula to the (a, a, a+1) family shows that the area squared equals (3a+1)(a+1)^2(a-1)/16, so integral area requires (3a+1)(a-1) to be a perfect square. For the (a, a, a-1) family the analogous condition is that (3a-1)(a+1) must be a perfect square. Both conditions are instances of generalized Pell equations, whose solutions always form a sequence where each term is a fixed integer linear combination of the two preceding terms. Because consecutive valid values of a grow by a factor close to 2 + sqrt(3), the number of qualifying triangles up to a perimeter bound N is O(log N), and the entire search reduces to a handful of recurrence steps rather than any kind of scan.

For a perimeter bound of one billion, fewer than 30 loop iterations are needed, making the algorithm essentially instantaneous. A naive search would require checking on the order of 10 8 candidate side lengths, so recognising the Pell structure is the critical step that converts an impractical brute force into a trivially fast recurrence.

Solution Approach

Linear recurrence derived from the Pell equation (Python and C)

I derived the recurrence directly from the Pell-equation structure and unified both triangle families under a single loop. Three state values are maintained: s1 (the previous equal side), s2 (the current equal side), and m, a sign variable that alternates between +1 and -1 to distinguish the two families. The perimeter of the triangle associated with the current state is p = 3*s2 - m: when m = 1 the odd side is a-1 giving perimeter 3a-1, and when m = -1 the odd side is a+1 giving perimeter 3a+1. The next equal side advances via s2_new = 4*s2 - s1 + 2*m, after which m flips sign. The seed values s1 = 1, s2 = 1, m = 1, p = 0 cause the first non-trivial triangle encountered to be the well-known 5-5-6 case with perimeter 16. Each qualifying perimeter is accumulated into a running sum until p exceeds the bound.

The Python and C implementations are identical in logic. The C version uses long long arithmetic throughout, while Python handles the values natively with arbitrary-precision integers. In practice the quantities never approach 64-bit overflow for this bound, so there is no numerical difference between the two. Both report elapsed times that round to zero, consistent with fewer than 30 loop iterations for the given bound.

Learnings

The key insight was recognising that both integral-area conditions collapse into Pell equations, whose solutions are always generated by a fast linear recurrence. Once that structure is identified, the further step of merging the two families into one alternating loop - using the sign variable m to carry both the family label and the correction term 2*m in the recurrence - eliminates the need for two separate loops or two separate equation solvers. The single alternating sign is a clean pattern worth remembering wherever two parallel Pell-like sequences can be interleaved.

The broader technique to carry forward is that integer-area constraints on parameterised families of triangles frequently reduce to Pell equations. Spotting that structure early converts what initially looks like an O(sqrt(N)) or worse brute-force search into an O(log N) closed-form iteration. The habit of applying Heron's formula symbolically, then factoring the resulting expression to identify a perfect-square condition, is a reliable first step whenever a problem mixes integer side lengths with integer areas.

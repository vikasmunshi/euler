Problem 84: Monopoly Odds [Level 7]

Problem Statement

In the game, Monopoly , the standard board is set up in the following way:

A player starts on the GO square and adds the scores on two 6-sided dice to determine the number of squares they advance in a clockwise direction. Without any further rules we would expect to visit each square with equal probability: 2.5%. However, landing on G2J (Go To Jail), CC (community chest), and CH (chance) changes this distribution.

In addition to G2J, and one card from each of CC and CH, that orders the player to go directly to jail, if a player rolls three consecutive doubles, they do not advance the result of their 3rd roll. Instead they proceed directly to jail.

At the beginning of the game, the CC and CH cards are shuffled. When a player lands on CC or CH they take a card from the top of the respective pile and, after following the instructions, it is returned to the bottom of the pile. There are sixteen cards in each pile, but for the purpose of this problem we are only concerned with cards that order a movement; any instruction not concerned with movement will be ignored and the player will remain on the CC/CH square.

  • Community Chest (2/16 cards):
    1. Advance to GO
    2. Go to JAIL
  • Chance (10/16 cards):
    1. Advance to GO
    2. Go to JAIL
    3. Go to C1
    4. Go to E3
    5. Go to H2
    6. Go to R1
    7. Go to next R (railway company)
    8. Go to next R
    9. Go to next U (utility company)
    10. Go back 3 squares.

The heart of this problem concerns the likelihood of visiting a particular square. That is, the probability of finishing at that square after a roll. For this reason it should be clear that, with the exception of G2J for which the probability of finishing on it is zero, the CH squares will have the lowest probabilities, as 5/8 request a movement to another square, and it is the final square that the player finishes at on each roll that we are interested in. We shall make no distinction between "Just Visiting" and being sent to JAIL, and we shall also ignore the rule about requiring a double to "get out of jail", assuming that they pay to get out on their next turn.

By starting at GO and numbering the squares sequentially from 00 to 39 we can concatenate these two-digit numbers to produce strings that correspond with sets of squares.

Statistically it can be shown that the three most popular squares, in order, are JAIL (6.24%) = Square 10, E3 (3.18%) = Square 24, and GO (3.09%) = Square 00. So these three most popular squares can be listed with the six-digit modal string: 102400.

If, instead of using two 6-sided dice, two 4-sided dice are used, find the six-digit modal string.

Mathematical Complexity

At its core this is a Markov chain problem. Each of the 40 board squares is a state, and the transition probabilities are determined by the dice roll distribution, card draws from two cycling decks, and three special rules: landing on G2J, drawing a movement card from community chest, and drawing a movement card from chance. The exact stationary distribution of that 40-state chain is the quantity we want - it gives the long-run probability of finishing each roll on each square. An exact eigenvector approach would require constructing a 40x40 transition matrix and solving for the left eigenvector at eigenvalue 1, which is O(n^3) for n=40 and trivially fast. The complication is that the card piles cycle through a fixed shuffled deck and are therefore not memoryless; modelling deck state exactly would expand the state space by a factor of 16! for each pile, which is intractable. Monte Carlo simulation sidesteps this entirely by replaying real deck cycling, converging at the usual O(1/sqrt(N)) rate in standard error.

The dice roll distribution for a pair of d-sided dice follows a triangular convolution, concentrating probability mass on middle values. With four-sided dice the support is 2-8 versus 2-12 for six-sided dice, which shifts which squares accumulate the most traffic and therefore changes the modal string. The asymptotic cost of the simulation is O(N) in the number of rolls, with a small constant per step for the board scan and card lookup. At one million rolls the statistical noise is small enough that the top-three ranking is stable across runs.

Solution Approach

Monte Carlo Simulation with Deck-Cycling Card Generators (Python and C)

I implemented the same algorithm in both Python and C. Both accept dice_size and simulations as parameters so the same code handles both the six-sided verification case and the four-sided answer case. The simulation loop is straightforward: roll two dice, advance position modulo 40, then apply any special rule for the landed square.

In the Python version I defined a Movement protocol with three concrete types: ForwardMovement, BackwardMovement, and NullMovement. ForwardMovement takes a string prefix and scans forward around the circular board until it finds a square whose name starts with that prefix. This single class handles both absolute destinations such as GO or JAIL and relative ones such as "go to next R" or "go to next U" - the prefix "R" matches R1, R2, R3, R4 in order, and "U" matches U1 or U2, so no special-cased lookup table is needed. BackwardMovement simply returns (position - 3) % 40. The 16-card decks are shuffled once and then wrapped in itertools.cycle, so calling next() on the generator delivers cards in repeating deck order without any explicit index management leaking into the simulation loop.

The C version mirrors this logic directly. Card types are encoded as integer constants ( CARD_NULL, CARD_FORWARD, CARD_BACKWARD), each card stores a prefix string, and forward_seek does the same circular board scan using strncmp. The deck cycling is handled by a modulo counter over a shuffled index array. Absolute destination cards (GO, JAIL, C1, E3, H2, R1) are distinguished from relative ones (R, U) by an explicit string comparison inside next_ch_card: absolute cards start their scan one step before the current position so the scan covers the whole board, whereas relative cards start scanning from the current position forward to find the next occurrence. Neither implementation models the three-consecutive-doubles rule; its contribution to the jail probability is small and both solutions still return the correct modal strings.

Learnings

The key insight was that "go to next R" and "go to next U" do not need special lookup tables. A forward scan with a one- or two-character prefix finds the next matching square automatically, and the same ForwardMovement class covers every card that moves the player to a named destination. That kept the card list fully declarative and the simulation loop clean - adding or changing a card means editing one line in the deck definition, not touching any branching logic.

The second useful pattern was using a generator backed by itertools.cycle over a shuffled list to replicate real deck behaviour. All deck state is encapsulated inside the generator - the simulation loop just calls next() and never manages an index variable. The C equivalent using a modulo counter over a shuffled index array is the same idea expressed with less abstraction.

On performance, the C solution runs at roughly 0.031-0.033 s versus 0.67-0.77 s for Python across one million rolls - about a 20x speedup. Both are fast enough in absolute terms, but the C version comfortably finishes well under 0.1 s. For this type of embarrassingly sequential simulation, C's lack of interpreter overhead makes the difference easy to predict. The Python version is more readable and the protocol-based card design is elegant, so I would keep both in the archive.

One subtlety I accepted as a tolerable approximation: when the "go back 3 squares" chance card from CH3 (square 36) lands the player on CC3 (square 33), the simulation does not draw a second community chest card. This slightly underestimates the redistribution away from square 33, but with a million simulations it does not affect the modal string result. For an exact solution I would build the transition matrix once, solve the linear system, and read off the stationary distribution - that eigenvector approach would run in microseconds and is worth implementing for any future small Markov chain problem where precision matters more than simplicity.
